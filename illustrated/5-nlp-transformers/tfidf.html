TF-IDF

Before embeddings, this is how you turn text into numbers — and it's still a strong, fast baseline for search and classification. The idea in one line: a word matters to a document if it appears often here but rarely elsewhere.

Two forces, multiplied

TF-IDF scores every (word, document) pair by multiplying two quantities:

  • Term Frequency (TF) — how often the word appears in this document. More mentions ⇒ more relevant. On its own, though, TF rewards filler: "the" and "of" dominate every document.
  • Inverse Document Frequency (IDF) — log(N / dfₜ), where dfₜ is how many of the N documents contain the word. This down-weights words that show up everywhere and boosts rare, distinctive ones. A word in every document has IDF ≈ 0; a word in just one has high IDF.

Multiply them — TF × IDF — and you get a score that's high only for words that are both frequent here and rare across the corpus: the words that actually characterise this document. Pick a document and see which words win:

From scores to vectors

Do this for every word and every document and you get a document vector — one TF-IDF number per vocabulary word. Now documents live in a shared space where you can compare them with cosine similarity: that's exactly how a classic search engine ranks pages against a query, and how a spam filter represents an email. The vectors are huge and sparse (mostly zeros, since any one document uses a tiny slice of the vocabulary), and — crucially — TF-IDF treats words as independent symbols: it has no idea that "car" and "automobile" are related. That semantic gap is exactly what word embeddings and, later, contextual embeddings were invented to close.

Takeaways: TF-IDF = TF (frequent in this doc) × IDF log(N/df) (rare across the corpus), so common filler scores ~0 and distinctive words score high. Stack the scores into a sparse document vector and compare docs with cosine similarity (search, classification). Fast and strong, but it ignores word meaning and order — the motivation for embeddings.
