merge & join

Real datasets live in pieces — customers in one table, their orders in another. Combining them on a shared key is exactly a SQL join, and the type of join decides what happens to rows that don't have a match on the other side. Pick the wrong one and you silently drop (or duplicate) data, so this is worth seeing rather than memorising.

The same key, four policies for non-matches

Two tables share an id column. pd.merge(left, right, on="id") lines up rows whose id matches and glues their columns together. The only real question is what to do with a row whose key has no partner on the other side — and that's what how= controls:

  • inner (the default) — keep only keys present in both. Safe, but quietly discards unmatched rows.
  • left — keep every left row; fill missing right-hand columns with NaN.
  • right — keep every right row; NaN on the left.
  • outer — keep everything from both; NaN wherever a side is missing.

Watch the key: the one-to-many gotcha

A merge matches every left row against every right row with the same key. If a key is unique on one side but repeats on the other (one customer, many orders) you get one result row per match — that's usually what you want. But if a key accidentally repeats on both sides, the merge multiplies them together and your row count explodes. After any merge, sanity-check the shape: did you get roughly the rows you expected? A surprise jump means a duplicated key.

concat is the other combiner, and it's different: merge joins side by side on a key (more columns), while pd.concat stacks frames on top of each other (more rows) — use it to append this month's data to last month's.

Takeaways: merge(..., on="key", how=...) aligns rows by a shared key. inner keeps only matched keys (can drop data); left/right keep one side fully with NaN fills; outer keeps all. Mind one-to-many keys — duplicates on both sides multiply rows. concat stacks rows; merge joins columns.
