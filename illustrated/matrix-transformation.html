Matrices as transformations

The single idea that unlocks linear algebra: a matrix isn't a grid of numbers — it's a function that moves space. Every 2×2 matrix takes the plane and stretches, rotates, shears, or flips it, and you can read exactly what it does straight off its columns.

linear transformationbasis vectors î ĵ columnsdeterminant = area singular

Read the columns: where î and ĵ land

Start with the two unit vectors that define the grid: î = (1, 0) pointing right and ĵ = (0, 1) pointing up. A linear transformation is completely determined by where these two land — everything else follows, because every other vector is just a combination of them. And here's the punchline: the columns of the matrix are exactly the landing spots. For [[a, b], [c, d]], î lands at the first column (a, c) and ĵ at the second (b, d). The determinant is the area it scales by

The blue parallelogram is where the original unit square ends up. Its area is the determinant, det = ad − bc. So the determinant has a concrete meaning: it's the factor by which the transformation scales every area. A determinant of 3 triples areas; 0.5 halves them; a negative determinant means space got flipped over (orientation reversed), like a mirror. And the critical case — det = 0 — means the transformation squashes the whole plane onto a line (or a point): it collapses a dimension. That's exactly when a matrix is singular and has no inverse, because once you've flattened space you can't unambiguously un-flatten it. (This is why "det ≠ 0" is the test for an invertible matrix and a unique solution to Ax = b.)

Why this is the foundation

Matrix multiplication is just doing one transformation then another; an eigenvector is a special direction the transformation only stretches without rotating; PCA finds the directions of greatest stretch in your data's covariance. All of it rests on this picture of a matrix as a moving grid. When a neural net multiplies by a weight matrix, it's transforming its input space exactly like this — just in many more dimensions.

Takeaways: a matrix moves space linearly; its columns are where î and ĵ land. The determinant ad − bc is the area-scaling factor — negative means a flip, and zero means space is collapsed onto a line (singular, non-invertible). Multiplication = composing transformations; eigenvectors are the directions left un-rotated.
