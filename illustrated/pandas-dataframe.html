Series, DataFrames & loc vs iloc

The two pandas objects you'll touch every day, and the two selectors people forever mix up. The whole thing rests on one idea: every row and column has a label, and the label is separate from the position.

Series vs DataFrame

A Series is a one-dimensional labeled array: a column of values plus an index (labels for each value). Think of it as a cross between a NumPy array and an ordered dict. A DataFrame is a two-dimensional table — really a dict of Series that share one index. Each column of a DataFrame is a Series; pull one out and that's exactly what you get back.

The piece that makes pandas more than "NumPy with names" is the index. It isn't just 0, 1, 2, … — it can be dates, ticker symbols, user IDs. That label-based index is what powers automatic alignment (operations match up rows by label, not position) and is the reason there are two ways to select: by label and by position.

.loc vs .iloc — the rule

  • .loc[] selects by LABEL — the actual index/column names. Slices are inclusive of the endpoint: df.loc['amy':'cara'] includes 'cara'.
  • .iloc[] selects by integer POSITION — 0-based, like NumPy/Python. Slices are exclusive of the endpoint: df.iloc[0:2] gives positions 0 and 1 only.

And the dimensionality rule that catches people: selecting a single row or single column returns a Series; selecting several returns a DataFrame; selecting one cell returns a scalar. Click each expression and watch what lights up — and read whether the result is a Series, a DataFrame, or a scalar:

Putting it to work

In practice you'll reach for these constantly, and the label/position split actually matters:

  • Grab a column to compute on: df['score'].mean() — a Series flows straight into NumPy-style math and aggregations.
  • Select features: df[['age', 'score']] — a list of names keeps it a DataFrame, ready to feed a model.
  • Filter rows by condition (boolean mask, just like NumPy): df[df['score'] > 80], or the explicit df.loc[df['score'] > 80, 'city'].
  • Position-based slicing when labels are messy: df.iloc[:5] for "the first five rows," regardless of what the index labels are.

Rule of thumb: prefer .loc when you're thinking in terms of what the rows are (a date, an ID), and .iloc when you mean where they are (first N, last N). Reaching into a DataFrame with a bare df[…] works for columns, but for rows or cells be explicit with .loc/.iloc to avoid ambiguity.

Takeaways: a Series is 1-D (values + index); a DataFrame is 2-D (a dict of aligned Series). .loc selects by label (slices inclusive), .iloc by position (slices exclusive). One row/column → Series; several → DataFrame; one cell → scalar.
