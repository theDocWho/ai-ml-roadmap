k-Means Clustering, Step by Step

An unsupervised algorithm that finds groups in unlabeled data by repeating two dead-simple moves — assign each point to its nearest center, then move each center to the middle of its points — until nothing changes.

unsupervisedassign → update centroidsinertiaBYO-4

The goal & the loop

You have points but no labels, and you suspect there are k natural groups. k-means finds them by maintaining k centroids (cluster centers) and alternating two steps — this is Lloyd's algorithm:

  1. Assign — color every point by whichever centroid is nearest (Euclidean distance). This carves the plane into regions, one per centroid.
  2. Update — move each centroid to the mean (center of gravity) of the points now assigned to it.

Repeat. Each round can only lower the total inertia — the sum of squared distances from points to their centroid — so it's guaranteed to converge. When an update moves the centroids essentially nowhere, you're done. Step it one move at a time and watch the centroids (◆) slide into the heart of each blob while the inertia drops:

How to read it: dots are data points colored by their current cluster; the large ◆ are the centroids. An Assign step recolors the dots (Voronoi regions); an Update step glides each ◆ to the average of its same-colored dots. The two steps chase each other until the centroids stop moving.

The catches you must know

  • It's sensitive to initialization. Different starting centroids can converge to different (worse) solutions — k-means only finds a local optimum. Click Re-init centroids a few times to see the final clustering change. The fix in practice is k-means++ (smart spread-out seeding) and running it several times, keeping the lowest inertia.
  • You must choose k yourself. It won't tell you how many clusters exist. The elbow method (plot inertia vs k and look for the bend) and the silhouette score help — but it's partly judgment.
  • It assumes round, similar-size clusters. Because it uses distance-to-mean, k-means struggles with elongated, nested, or very unequal clusters (try forcing the wrong k). For those, reach for DBSCAN or Gaussian Mixture Models.
  • Scale your features first. Distance is dominated by large-range features, so standardize before clustering (see per-column standardization).
Takeaways: k-means alternates assign (point → nearest centroid) and update (centroid → mean of its points), lowering inertia until it converges to a local optimum. Pick k yourself (elbow/silhouette), seed smartly (k-means++) and run several times, scale your features, and remember it likes round clusters. Build it from scratch in BYO-4.
