Bayes' theorem

How to update a belief when new evidence arrives — the engine behind Naive Bayes, spam filters, medical diagnosis, and a surprising amount of human-judgment error. The formula is short; the intuition is the famous "a positive test doesn't mean what you think."

priorlikelihood posteriorbase rate false positives

The formula, in plain words

Bayes' theorem rearranges conditional probability into an update rule:

P(disease | +)  =  P(+ | disease) · P(disease)  /  P(+)
  • prior P(disease) — what you believed before the test (here, how common the disease is — the base rate).
  • likelihood P(+ | disease) — how the evidence behaves if the hypothesis is true (the test's sensitivity).
  • evidence P(+) — how often the evidence shows up overall (true and false positives), which normalises the result.
  • posterior P(disease | +) — your updated belief after seeing the evidence.

See it with a population of 1,000

The cleanest way to feel Bayes is to count actual people. Set how common the disease is and how accurate the test is, then look at the 1,000 people: of everyone who tests positive, what fraction is actually sick? That fraction is the posterior — and it's often shockingly low.

The base-rate trap

Crank the disease down to 1% with a 99%-accurate test and the posterior still lands far below what intuition screams — frequently below 50%. Why? When the disease is rare, the healthy group is so much larger that even a tiny false-positive rate produces a flood of false positives that can swamp the few true ones. The evidence still updates your belief (from 1% up to maybe 16%), it just doesn't make it a certainty. This is the base-rate fallacy, and Bayes' theorem is the cure: it forces the prior into the calculation instead of letting you ignore it.

Takeaways: posterior ∝ likelihood × prior, normalised by the total evidence. A test result must be weighed against the base rate: when the prior is small, even an accurate test yields mostly false positives, so P(disease|+) stays low. Updating beliefs with evidence — never from the likelihood alone — is the whole game. (Exam Q12/Q13.)
